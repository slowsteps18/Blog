"You're lucky because you can negative gear."
"You're negative gearing? You must be loaded."

I'm sure you've heard comments to this tune again and again, especially during the last federal election, along with cries to remove franking credits.

I cringe when people talk negative gearing as more often than not, it's clear many do not quite understand what it means. Either that or facts are cherry picked to promote their view.

Here is my attempt at a succinct explanation, politics aside.

What does positive/negative gearing really mean?

An IP (investment property) is essentially a business, meaning it's used to try to generate a profit. Like a business, it has expenses which can be claimed as deductions from our income.

Negative gearing

If your investment property costs you more money than it makes you in a financial year, you have a negatively geared property.

Positive gearing

Conversely, if your investment property makes you more money than it costs in the financial year, you have a positively geared property.

A Simplified Example

Assume the following; you have an investment property, rented every day, with mortage of $700k at ~4%p.a.

Rental income $15,000
Property manager fees (include re-lease). -$800
Lending costs (interest/bank fees) -$18,000
Rates -$1800
Water Charges -$500
Miscellaneous repairs -$1000
Total out of pocket $9100

You can see in this example, the Rental Provider is out of pocket $9100. You're reading correctly, $9100 ↓. This is a negatively geared situation, and to clarify:

  1. The investor will not recieve the full $9100 back on tax. The amount will be removed from their taxable income. E.g. if you earned $100k, you would only pay tax on $90,900.
  2. The interest charges claimed are just that, you cannot claim the mortgage repayments. E.g. The repayment amount in a year for this example would be closer to $33,000, where as the claimable about (interest) is only $18,000.

Loaded? Not quite.

You can quickly see how much it could potentially cost a Rental Provider to own an IP annually. Yes the negative gearing absolutely helps mitigate risk and make the investment better; but I'd take positively geared property over it any day of the week.
